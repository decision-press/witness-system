To influence inactive Catholics, personal outreach, sincere conversations, and prayer are key. The most effective efforts focus on repairing broken relationships and addressing the specific issues that led them to drift away, rather than relying on guilt or judgment. 

Individual outreach

·  Pray for them. The most fundamental step is to pray for the person you have in mind. Ask God for his guidance and the grace to reach their heart.  Chorbishop Alfred and Father Chris - would you supply us with a specific prayer for our inactive Catholics, that we could all use regularly?

·  Share your own story. Reflect on your own faith journey and be prepared to share how the Catholic faith has helped you through personal challenges. This can be more powerful than just reciting Church teachings.

·  Extend a gentle invitation. After laying a foundation of trust, extend a personal, low-pressure invitation. Instead of inviting them to Mass right away, consider:

A parish social event, like a picnic or festival.
Eucharistic adoration, a quiet and less formal setting.
A local adult education event.
A low-key dinner with a priest or deacon, if they are open to it.
Motivational Speaker on the subject
A home mass, conducted in the home of an inactive Catholic with their entire family
·  Don't rely on guilt. Guilt can cause a person to become more resistant and paralyzed. Focus on encouragement and the positive aspects of the faith instead. 

Parish-based initiatives

For parishes seeking to welcome back inactive members, here are some helpful strategies:

·  Form an outreach team. Recruit volunteers, particularly former inactive Catholics, who can serve as a sensitive and empathetic bridge to those outside the Church.

·  Use existing programs. Implement or promote proven programs, such as Paulist Evangelization Ministries' "Awakening Faith," which is a small-group process designed for returning Catholics.

·  Create inviting parish environments. Make sure all aspects of parish life are welcoming. A 2014 survey in the Diocese of Springfield revealed that many former Catholics left because they found Masses boring, the atmosphere impersonal, or the clergy uninspiring.

·  Follow up. After a person attends a parish event, provide continued support through consistent follow-ups. Offer ongoing catechetical resources and keep them connected with the parish community.

·  Lead with humility. Acknowledge that the Church has made mistakes and that everyone struggles with their faith at some point. Pope Francis models this approach by welcoming those who have felt marginalized by the Church, such as divorced and remarried Catholics. 

Ultimately, the goal is not to "win an argument" but to help a person feel loved and accepted as a child of God, recognizing that a person's return is a journey guided by God's grace.
