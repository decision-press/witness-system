Key themes & take-aways
Here are some of the driving thoughts in the passage (yes — I dragged myself off the couch for this).
    1. Permanence of the promise (vv. 15-18)
        ◦ Paul uses an analogy: once a human will or covenant is ratified, it cannot be undone. (v.15) (BibleRef.com)
        ◦ The promises made to Abraham are of this nature — they weren’t made to “descendants” in the plural but to one offspring (“your descendant”), which Paul interprets as Christ. (v.16) (sharperiron.org)
        ◦ The point: the law (given later) does not annul God’s covenant with Abraham. (v.17-18) (Bible Hub)
        ◦ If the inheritance (blessing) depended on law-keeping, then it would no longer depend on promise. But it comes by promise. (v.18) (Holy Joys)
    2. Purpose of the law (vv. 19-21)
        ◦ Paul anticipates the question: “If the promise stands, then why was the law given?” (v.19) He answers: the law was added “because of transgressions,” until the coming of the promised offspring. (gospellight.sg)
        ◦ The law was delivered through angels and a mediator (v.19b-20). The promise, however, is direct from God; therefore the law is of different status. (Enduring Word)
        ◦ The law is not opposed to God’s promises (v.21) — but if a law could bring life, righteousness would have come via the law. Paul says it doesn’t. (sharperiron.org)
    3. Human condition, faith, and the promise (v. 22)
        ◦ Scripture “confined all things under sin,” so that the promise — by faith in Christ — might be given to those who believe (v.22). This is not pessimism for its own sake, but a setup: the law shows human inability, the promise gives access by faith. (Enduring Word)
        ◦ In other words: The law diagnoses, the promise provides — through faith.

Implications / reflection
    • The covenant God made with Abraham is foundational; Paul emphasizes that it stands independent of the law. So one implication: Christian identity isn’t based primarily on law-obedience but on faith in the promise.
    • The law’s role isn’t to replace the promise or to serve as the primary path to righteousness, but to highlight our need, our limitation, and prepare for the coming of Christ.
    • Faith becomes the means by which one participates in the promise — not “works of the law.” This doesn’t dismiss moral law (that would collapse the text) but shifts the ground of blessing.
    • For believers: the passage can be an encouragement that God’s promises are steadfast. It can also be a caution against relying on mere law-keeping as the ground of acceptance before God.

Regex to remove orphaned footnote/concordance numbers
(?<=\n)\d{1,2}(?=\s)
Replace with empty string.

(?<=\s)\d{1,2}(?=\s[A-Z])
Replace with empty.
This removes numbers that sit between line breaks or mid-sentence, but it leaves verse numbers at the start of a verse line intact.
If you want the nuclear option:

Removes any line that contains only a number.
